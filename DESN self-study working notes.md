## Final Answers

### 1. Program Philosophy and Graduate Outcomes (1,448/1,500 chars)

Design (DESN) prepares adaptive, socially conscious designers who can think, make, critique, and present across changing media environments. The Bachelor of Design (BDes) in Visual Communication Design treats design as a disciplined process rather than a styling exercise: students build visual and typographic fluency, technical range from print to web, motion, UX, and AI-assisted tools, and working methods that include systems thinking, design thinking, and inclusive design. Five program learning outcomes carry through the required curriculum from the first foundations course to the community-partner capstone: Visual Vocabulary, Technical Skills, Design Methodology, Career Readiness, and Well-Being — employing principles of well-being to self, others, and community. Graduates leave with a professional portfolio, real client experience, and the ability to define problems, research audiences, prototype, respond to critique, and present work publicly. After the degree, we want them to practice design as a civic and economic act — responsible to real audiences, clients, and communities — and to sustain creative careers in graphic design, UX/product design, web and front-end design, motion, game design, and related fields. Just as important, we want them to carry the program's culture of well-being and mutual support into the profession: designers who mentor others, serve their region, and keep learning as the discipline evolves.

### 2. EWU Mission, Vision, and Values (1,493/1,500 chars)

DESN is a working model of EWU's 2024-2029 strategic plan. The university's mission promises an inclusive, equitable, and transformative learning experience; its vision emphasizes applied learning in high-demand fields; and EWU brands itself as Washington's only polytechnic, built on learning by doing. That is DESN's method: every studio is hands-on, project-based, and career-connected, and our capstone-anchored studio teams have designed for nonprofit, civic, tribal, educational, and environmental partners across the region. The program advances the university's values. Student Success and Accessibility: in our 2021-22 program data, 44% of our students were first-generation and roughly 38% were students of color; the department pays for students' Adobe Creative Cloud subscriptions and caps studios at 24. Belonging: equity and inclusion are written into our learning outcomes, and faculty have served as ODEI Diversity Fellows and as affiliated Gender, Women's & Sexuality Studies faculty. Regional Impact: graduates fill Washington's creative-workforce demand — arts and cultural production is 9.8% of state GDP, the nation's highest share (BEA/NEA, 2023). Sustainability: a $40,000 grant from regional firm McKinstry funded a year-long student installation interpreting the zero-energy mission of the Catalyst Building, the program's Spokane home from 2020 to 2026. Taken together, DESN contributes directly to the social, economic, and cultural vitality of the Inland Northwest.

### 3. Recent Alumni Examples (1,373/1,500 chars)

Three recent graduates show where DESN degrees go. Gibelly Lopez-Benicio (BDes 2025, summa cum laude) was working as a graphic designer at Ignitium, in the emerging marketing-technology sector, within a year of graduation — our newest graduates enter professional practice quickly. Savannah Johnson (BDes 2021) works as a UX product designer, a specialty the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics projects will grow much faster than average — 7% for web developers and digital designers, the category covering UX/UI, versus 3% across all occupations (2024-34). Chance Woudenberg (BDes 2021, UX/UI and web design certificate) built his career, per our 2023 alumni survey, as a graphic designer and front-end developer with a full-service marketing agency, spanning print, digital, and interactive web work. Beyond these three, the survey documented graduates from the same window working as a page designer/copy editor at The Spokesman-Review and a content designer maintaining EWU's web presence, while earlier graduates have held senior design roles — a Senior Product Designer at Bitwarden, the Senior Graphic Designer for King County Elections. Regional demand is structural: many studios and agencies on our industry contact list are staffed by program alumni, including the co-owner of a Spokane design and marketing firm — our graduates are the regional creative workforce.

### 4. Internal Demand (1,332/1,500 chars)

DESN functions as design infrastructure for EWU. In our most recent program-review data, service courses accounted for more than a quarter of the department's student credit hours, and DESN classes required by other programs filled at an average of 86%. Public Relations majors complete one course chosen from six DESN options; Computer Science programs have required four DESN courses (216, 336, 368, 446); Technical Communication requires three; Music Technology, Creative Writing, and the Web Application Development minor also build DESN coursework into their curricula. The department contributes to General Education directly: the DESN 490 community-partner capstone counts toward General Education as well as the major, and DESN 359, Global Histories of Design, satisfies EWU's Diversity requirement and was offered in partnership with the Honors program beginning fall 2023. Our seven new design minors — from Graphic Design and Web Development to Game Design — give students in any major a structured design credential. Internal demand extends past coursework: DESN students have worked as designers in roughly 28 campus offices, from University Marketing & Communications to Admissions, Libraries, Athletics, and The Easterner, and the department's 4D Lab extends this service role with printing and fabrication resources.

### 5. Efficiency, Growth, and Barriers (1,495/1,500 chars)

DESN has repeatedly restructured for efficiency and growth. Guided by our assessment findings, we converted studio courses from 4 to 5 credits — majors take fewer, deeper courses, shortening time-to-degree and strengthening portfolios — and rebuilt the BDes around a 90-credit structure. We have converted certificates into minors and created new ones — seven design minors in all (Graphic Design, Web Development, User Experience Design, Interaction Design, Animation & Motion Design, Game Design, and Photography) — documented in our renewed 2025 articulation with Spokane Falls Community College, which transfers AAS/T graphic-design graduates directly into the BDes; in our 2021-22 financials, the UX certificate returned the department's highest net revenue per credit hour. Our barriers are structural. First, location: the 2020 move to the Catalyst Building opened transportation and access gaps between Cheney student services and Spokane studios; in summer 2026 the program relocated to Isle Hall in Cheney — faculty offices moved in June, and the building is being built out for classes starting fall 2026 — reuniting design students with campus services. Second, staffing: as of fall 2023 only four of nine full-time faculty were tenure-line, a ratio we have yet to rebalance — concentrating advising and governance on a few people. Sustained growth requires a new tenure-track line and advising support; we pursue both while protecting the small-studio model that drives our quality.

### 6. Applied Learning (1,368/1,500 chars)

Applied learning is not a component of DESN — it is the pedagogy, aligned with EWU's polytechnic identity and the strategic plan's goal of experiential learning across undergraduate curricula. Studios produce real work for real audiences through research, prototyping, critique, and public presentation. Since 2017 our studios — anchored by the senior capstone — have carried out dozens of community-partner client engagements, nearly every quarter, working with the Kalispel Tribe, Spokane Public Libraries, City of Spokane Public Works, Feast World Kitchen, and the League of Women Voters, among others; advanced-studio students have created work for audiences as large as 1,300 attendees at the Women Helping Women Fund's annual fundraiser. The program maintains an internship network of 60+ host employers — Avista, Zak Designs, Gravity Jack, Seven2 — and students fill design roles across campus. The path from coursework to employment is programmatic: an alumni speaker series and the Low Key Level Up senior portfolio night move students directly toward the job market. Going forward, applied learning widens: our seven design minors extend client-based studio work to non-majors, DESN 374 (AI + Design) and new game-design courses carry industry-relevant projects, and professional-continuing-education certificates bring applied training to working designers.

### 7. Other Information (1,400/1,500 chars)

Three things round out the picture. First, lineage and reach: DESN descends from a departmental line at EWU dating to 1917 — the design degree itself emerged in the early 1960s — and is, to our knowledge, home to the only Bachelor of Design (BDes) degree in Eastern Washington. More than 850 alumni have completed the program, including nearly 250 graduates across 2021-2025; the 2025 class alone included 45 BDes graduates, 26 with Latin honors, plus 38 certificate completions (earned by 26 students, many of them the same graduates). Second, pipeline: DESN is an on-ramp into creative careers for young people who would not otherwise have professional design networks or portfolio support — hosting NEWTech High School visits, running summer design camps for grades 6-8 (three camps in 2025), and articulating directly with Spokane Falls Community College. Third, momentum with stability: faculty present nationally on AI and design education, including for AIGA; the 4D Lab's printing and fabrication services now serve paying clients such as a regional health-education partner; and faculty retention is exceptional — as of our 2023 review, only two departures in five years from the team known as the 'DESN FMLY.' DESN is a small department that functions as the region's design hub: a professional degree, a community design resource, and an engine for the Inland Northwest's creative economy.
