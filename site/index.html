Verified Question 1

Program philosophy and graduate outcomes

Explain the philosophy of the program and what graduates should accomplish after they obtain their degree.

Final draft

Design (DESN) prepares adaptive, socially conscious designers who can think, make, critique, and present across changing media environments. The Bachelor of Design (BDes) in Visual Communication Design treats design as a disciplined process rather than a styling exercise: students build visual and typographic fluency, technical range from print to web, motion, UX, and AI-assisted tools, and working methods that include systems thinking, design thinking, and inclusive design. Five program learning outcomes — Visual Vocabulary, Technical Skills, Design Methodology, Career Readiness, and Well-Being — carry through the required curriculum from the first foundations course to the community-partner capstone. Graduates leave with a professional portfolio, real client experience, and the ability to define problems, research audiences, prototype, respond to critique, and present work publicly. After the degree, we want them to practice design as a civic and economic act — responsible to real audiences, clients, and communities — and to sustain creative careers in graphic design, UX/product design, web and front-end design, motion, game design, and related fields. Just as important, we want them to carry the program's culture of well-being and mutual support into the profession: designers who mentor others, serve their region, and keep learning as the discipline evolves.

Verified Question 2

EWU mission, vision, and values

How the degree aligns with EWU's mission, vision, and values, and contributes to social, economic, or cultural objectives.

Final draft

DESN is a working model of EWU's 2024-2029 strategic plan. The university's mission promises an inclusive, equitable, and transformative learning experience; its vision emphasizes applied learning in high-demand fields; and EWU brands itself as Washington's only polytechnic, built on learning by doing. That is DESN's method: every studio is hands-on, project-based, and career-connected, and our senior capstone teams design for nonprofit, civic, tribal, educational, and environmental partners across the region. The program advances the university's values. Student Success and Accessibility: per our most recent program data, 44% of our students are first-generation and roughly 38% are students of color; the department pays for students' Adobe Creative Cloud subscriptions and caps studios at 24, so every student gets professional tools and close mentorship. Belonging: equity and inclusion are written into our learning outcomes, and faculty have served as ODEI Diversity Fellows and as affiliated Gender, Women's & Sexuality Studies faculty. Regional Impact: graduates fill regional creative-workforce demand in a state whose creative economy accounts for roughly a tenth of its GDP. Sustainability: a $40,000 grant from regional firm McKinstry funded a year-long student installation interpreting the zero-energy mission of the Catalyst Building, our Spokane home. DESN contributes directly to the social, economic, and cultural vitality of the Inland Northwest.

Confirm alumni Question 3

Recent alumni examples

Three examples of graduates from the past five years contributing to employment demand, trends, emerging markets, and regional/state/national needs.

Final draft

Graduates from the past five years are meeting employment demand in design fields the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics projects to grow much faster than average — our 2023 program analysis cited 17-23% growth for UX design and web development. Three examples: Savannah Johnson (BDes 2021) works as a UX product designer, the specialty at the center of that projected growth. Chance Woudenberg (BDes 2021, UX and web design certificates) built his career, per our 2023 alumni survey, as a graphic designer and front-end developer with a full-service marketing agency, spanning print, digital, and interactive web work. Gibelly Lopez-Benicio (BDes 2025, summa cum laude) moved from graduation into the emerging marketing-technology market as a graphic designer at Ignitium — evidence that our newest graduates enter professional practice quickly. Beyond these three, that survey documented graduates from the same window working as a page designer/copy editor at The Spokesman-Review and a content designer maintaining EWU's web presence, while earlier graduates have held senior product-design roles at organizations including Bitwarden and Washington's King County Elections. Regional demand is also structural: many of the studios and agencies on our industry contact list are staffed by program alumni, including the co-owner of a Spokane design and marketing firm — our graduates are the regional creative workforce.

Verified Question 4

Internal demand

Gen Ed, service courses, and collaborations with other EWU programs.

Final draft

DESN functions as design infrastructure for EWU. In our most recent program-review data, service courses accounted for more than a quarter of the department's student credit hours, and DESN classes required by other programs filled at an average of 86%. Public Relations majors complete one course chosen from six DESN options; Computer Science programs have required four DESN courses (216, 336, 368, 446); Technical Communication requires three; Music Technology, Creative Writing, and the Web Application Development minor also build DESN coursework into their curricula. The department contributes to General Education directly: the DESN 490 community-partner capstone counts toward General Education as well as the major, and DESN 359, Global Histories of Design, satisfies EWU's Diversity requirement and has been offered in partnership with the Honors program since fall 2023. Our seven new design minors — from Graphic Design and Web Development to Game Design — give students in any major a structured design credential. Internal demand extends past coursework: DESN students have worked as designers in roughly 28 campus offices, from University Marketing & Communications (whose creative staff has long been drawn largely from program alumni) to Admissions, Libraries, Athletics, and The Easterner, and the Catalyst 4D Lab extends this service role with printing and fabrication resources.

Verified Question 5

Efficiency, growth, and barriers

Efficiency and growth measures taken or planned, and the barriers the program faces.

Final draft

DESN has repeatedly restructured for efficiency and growth. Guided by our assessment findings, we converted studio courses from 4 to 5 credits — majors take fewer, deeper courses, shortening time-to-degree and strengthening portfolios — and rebuilt the BDes around a 90-credit structure. We have reshaped certificates into a family of seven minors — Graphic Design, Web Development, User Experience, Interaction Design, Animation & Motion Design, Game Design, and Photography — documented in our renewed 2025 articulation with Spokane Falls Community College, while adding courses such as DESN 374 (AI + Design) and new game-design offerings. That articulation transfers associate-degree (AAS/T) graphic design graduates directly into the BDes, and new professional-continuing-education certificates add enrollment and revenue; in our 2021-22 program financials, the UX certificate returned the department's highest net revenue per credit hour. Our barriers are structural. First, location: the 2020 move to the Catalyst Building created transportation and access gaps between Cheney student services and Spokane studios, affecting recruitment and degree pathways. Second, staffing: as of fall 2023 only four of nine full-time faculty were tenure-line, a ratio we have yet to rebalance — concentrating advising and governance on a few people. Sustained growth requires a new tenure-track line and advising support; we pursue both while protecting the small-studio model that drives our quality.

Verified Question 6

Applied learning

How the program has/will implement applied learning, including business, regional, and societal needs.

Final draft

Applied learning is not a component of DESN — it is the pedagogy, aligned with EWU's polytechnic identity and the strategic plan's goal of experiential learning across undergraduate curricula. Studios produce real work for real audiences through research, prototyping, critique, and public presentation. Since 2017 our studios — anchored by the senior capstone — have paired student teams with community-partner clients nearly every quarter — the Kalispel Tribe, Spokane Public Libraries, City of Spokane Public Works, Feast World Kitchen, and the League of Women Voters among them — and advanced-studio students created artwork for the Women Helping Women Fund's 1,300-attendee annual fundraiser. The program maintains an internship network of 60+ host employers (Avista, Zak Designs, Gravity Jack, Seven2), and students staff design jobs across campus. The path from coursework to employment is programmatic: an alumni speaker series, the Low Key Level Up senior portfolio night, and the LEVEL UP Creative Career Fair, launched in May 2025 at the Catalyst Building, the program's Spokane facility. Going forward, applied learning widens: our seven new design minors extend client-based studio work to non-majors, DESN 374 (AI + Design) and new game-design courses carry industry-relevant projects, and professional-continuing-education certificates bring applied training to working designers — applied learning built around business, regional, and societal demand.

Verified Question 7

Other information

Anything else that helps reviewers understand the program.

Final draft

Three things round out the picture. First, lineage and reach: DESN descends from a departmental line at EWU dating to 1917, with the design degree itself emerging in the early 1960s; today it is, to our knowledge, the only bachelor's degree in design offered in Eastern Washington. More than 850 alumni have completed the program, including nearly 250 graduates across 2021-2025; the 2025 class alone included 45 BDes graduates, 26 with Latin honors, plus 38 certificate completions (earned by 26 students, many of them the same graduates). Second, pipeline: we are an on-ramp into creative careers — hosting NEWTech High School visits at Catalyst, running summer design camps for grades 6-8 (three camps in 2025), and articulating directly with Spokane Falls Community College — for young people who would not otherwise have professional design networks or portfolio support. Third, momentum with stability: faculty present nationally on AI and design education, including for AIGA, the professional association for design; the 4D Lab's printing and fabrication services now serve paying external clients such as a regional health-education partner; and faculty retention is exceptional — as of our 2023 review, only two departures in five years from the team known as the 'DESN FMLY.' DESN is a small department that functions as the region's design hub: a professional degree, a community design resource, and an engine for the Inland Northwest's creative economy.
