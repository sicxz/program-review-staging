Draft-ready Question 1

Program philosophy and graduate outcomes

Describe the program's philosophy and what graduates should accomplish after earning the degree.

Expanded draft

DESN prepares students to become adaptive, socially conscious visual designers who can think, make, critique, and present across changing media environments. The program treats design as a disciplined process: students learn visual vocabulary, typography, image-making, systems thinking, research, audience analysis, prototyping, technical production, and reflection. Studio courses ask students to move between analog craft, digital tools, human-centered methods, collaboration, and public presentation. Graduates should leave with a professional portfolio and an ability to define problems, test solutions, respond to feedback, and communicate responsibly with real audiences, clients, and communities. They should also understand design as a civic and economic practice, not only a styling practice, and be prepared for careers in graphic design, UX/product design, web/front-end design, motion, media, marketing, and related creative fields.

Draft-ready Question 2

EWU mission, vision, and values

Explain how the program aligns with EWU's mission, vision, values, and social/economic/cultural objectives.

Expanded draft

DESN aligns with EWU's mission by making applied, inclusive, career-connected learning the center of the curriculum. The program serves many first-generation, transfer, regional, and working students by giving them access to professional design tools, critique culture, portfolio development, internships, and client-based work. Studio projects translate EWU values into practice: students design for nonprofit, civic, educational, cultural, environmental, and campus partners; they address accessibility, sustainability, audience needs, and social impact; and they learn to collaborate across differences. The program supports regional workforce development by preparing graduates for high-demand visual communication, UX, web, marketing, and digital media roles. It also contributes to EWU's cultural and economic objectives by making student work visible in the community, connecting students to employers, and producing design capacity for campus and regional partners.

Draft-ready Question 4

Internal demand

Describe General Education, service courses, and collaborations with other EWU programs.

Expanded draft

DESN serves internal demand in two ways: it supports majors in the BDes pathway and it provides design capacity, coursework, and applied-learning partnerships for other parts of EWU. Students from Public Relations, Computer Science, Technical Communication, Honors, Sustainability, Biology, Business, Social Work, GWSS, and other areas intersect with DESN through required or optional coursework, cross-listed classes, service courses, interdisciplinary projects, and campus employment. The program also supports EWU units through student design work for offices, events, publications, campaigns, and community-facing projects. The 4D Lab and related making/print resources extend this internal service role by helping students and programs produce visual, physical, and digital communication artifacts. In that sense, DESN is not an isolated major; it is part of the university's communication, recruitment, outreach, research, and student-success infrastructure.

Draft-ready Question 5

Efficiency, growth, and barriers

Discuss efficiency, growth measures, planned changes, and barriers.

Expanded draft

DESN has pursued efficiency and growth through curriculum revision, conversion to 5-credit course structures, certificate development, transfer/articulation pathways, relocation and programming at Catalyst, applied-learning expansion, and stronger links to industry/community partners. The program has also identified changes that can help it respond to student and market demand: clarifying BDes requirements, considering whether certificates should become minors, updating pathways in UX, front-end/web, motion, design engineering, and graphic design, and strengthening portfolio-to-employment transitions. The main barriers are structural rather than lack of demand: advising load, the balance of tenure-track and lecturer staffing, software/equipment/lab costs, transportation and student-service access between Cheney and Catalyst, and technical support for labs and the 4D Lab. Continued growth will depend on protecting studio quality while updating curriculum for emerging technologies, accessibility, AI, product design, and regional workforce needs.

Draft-ready Question 6

Applied learning

Explain how applied learning is implemented, including business, regional, and societal needs.

Expanded draft

Applied learning is the program's core method, not a supplemental activity. DESN students learn by making work for real audiences, presenting to peers and clients, revising through critique, and reflecting on both process and impact. Across studios, capstones, internships, directed studies, professional practice, student employment, and lab-based production, students practice research, audience analysis, concept development, prototyping, technical execution, project management, and presentation. Community and campus projects connect students with regional business, civic, nonprofit, educational, cultural, and environmental partners such as public agencies, libraries, sustainability offices, foundations, transit and civic organizations, and local cultural groups. These experiences respond to regional and societal needs while giving students portfolio evidence, professional confidence, and a clearer transition into employment.

Draft-ready Question 7

Other information

Add anything else reviewers should understand about the program.

Expanded draft

DESN's value is larger than the number of majors it serves. The program functions as a regional design hub: a professional BDes pathway, a collaborator for campus units, a source of design capacity for nonprofits and public partners, a maker/print/fabrication resource, and a bridge between students and the creative economy. It is also an access point for first-generation, transfer, and regional students who may not otherwise have professional design networks, critique culture, current software/hardware, or portfolio support. Recent activity shows momentum in AI, UX/product design, front-end design, motion/animation, game-related work, Risograph and print culture, sustainability, community design, and student research. The program's future opportunity is to keep that breadth coherent: preserve the hands-on studio foundation while investing in staffing, advising, lab support, industry partnerships, and curricular pathways that reflect where design practice is moving.
